About

01

My path into biomedical engineering began with two interests that have always shaped the way I think: chemical engineering and medicine.

As an undergraduate at the University of New Mexico, I studied chemical engineering while also pursuing pre-medical coursework, which gave me a strong foundation in materials, transport, chemistry, biology, and human health. I was drawn to the way engineering could be used not only to understand complex systems, but to create tools that help diagnose, treat, and improve disease outcomes.

Today, my PhD work at CU Boulder allows me to bridge those interests directly. I develop and characterize medical device platforms designed to support regenerative therapies and drug discovery for osteoarthritis. My research focuses on building a joint-on-chip system that recreates key features of the joint microenvironment, including cartilage-bone communication, biological signaling, and mechanical loading.

My work includes device development, mechanical characterization, experimental validation, and computational modeling using Abaqus. By designing the device, testing its performance, and modeling how it behaves under load, I aim to create a platform that can help researchers better understand osteoarthritis progression and evaluate future therapeutic strategies.

I'm motivated by problems where engineering has a direct connection to human health—where materials, mechanics, biology, and medicine come together to create something useful, measurable, and impactful.

Research & Experience

03

August 2023 – Present

Graduate Researcher

Medical Device Development for Osteoarthritis Research | University of Colorado Boulder

Developing and characterizing a medical device platform designed to support regenerative therapies and drug discovery for osteoarthritis.

My research focuses on a joint-on-chip system that models communication between cartilage and bone in a controlled microscale environment. The platform is designed to recreate important features of the joint microenvironment, including biological signaling and mechanical loading, to better understand early osteoarthritis disease processes.

I am responsible for advancing the device's mechanical component through design, characterization, validation, and computational modeling. This includes evaluating device performance experimentally and using Abaqus to model mechanical behavior, optimize design decisions, and better understand how the system responds under physiologically relevant loading conditions.

Over the past year, I have helped prepare the device for patenting by characterizing and validating its mechanical performance. I have also explored new design strategies beyond the original pneumatic approach, including magnetic PDMS-based concepts for controlled mechanical stimulation.

Key Achievement: Collaborative Research Contribution — On-Chip Membrane Characterization

Contributed to a collaborative project characterizing membranes for integration into the joint-on-chip system. This work led to the submission of a manuscript on which I served as second author.
